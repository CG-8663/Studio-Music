## Customer Pain Points and Needs

### Customer Challenges and Frustrations

**The AI music production market in 2026 is defined by a central tension: the tools are powerful enough to be useful but not trustworthy enough to be relied upon.**

**1. Quality Inconsistency — "The Generic Sound Problem"**
More than a quarter of producers report that AI tools simply aren't of sufficient quality for professional use. Specific issues include:
- Quantization artifacts, timing drifts, and compression overshoot making tracks sound mechanical
- AI fails to interpret emotional nuance, timing variation, or harmonic intent
- Compositional logic struggles with progression consistency — melodies loop awkwardly or lose coherence beyond short sequences
- Unnatural vocal transitions, repetitive song structures, lack of dynamic expressiveness
- Output frequently described as **"generic or bland"** — palatable but lacking artistic depth

**Impact:** This is the #1 barrier to professional adoption and the strongest validation of Major 72's "professional judgment" thesis. The tools work, but the results need a human ear.

_Sources: [Soundverse Quality Issues](https://www.soundverse.ai/blog/article/ai-music-quality-issues-common-problems-solutions-1044), [Sound On Sound](https://www.soundonsound.com/music-business/ai-music-tech-2026), [Cornell Daily Sun](https://www.cornellsun.com/article/2024/12/the-problem-with-ai-generated-music)_

**2. Creative Identity Threat — "Musical Sameness"**
- **>33% of producers** worry AI will compromise their creative intent or undermine their individuality
- **80% of producers** are neutral-to-negative on AI — not hostile, but deeply cautious
- A Tracklib study found **over 80% of producers are against AI-generated songs** (though this conflates "full AI generation" with "AI-assisted production")
- Producers fear a flood of competent but interchangeable tracks that erode what makes their work distinctive

**Impact:** Producers will only adopt AI tools that **protect and amplify** their unique sound, not replace it. Major 72's Creative Modes directly address this by encoding a specific producer's aesthetic rather than defaulting to a generic output.

_Sources: [MusicTech](https://musictech.com/news/industry/music-producers-are-rejecting-ai-study-reveals-over-80-of-producers-are-against-ai-generated-songs/), [Sonarworks Survey](https://www.sonarworks.com/blog/research/future-music-production-human-producer-survey-2026), [AI Journal](https://aijourn.com/ai-music-production-tools-2026-how-producers-use-ai-to-cut-friction-in-modern-music-workflows/)_

**3. The AI Slop Crisis — Streaming Platforms Drowning**
- **50,000 AI-generated tracks uploaded daily** to Deezer alone (34% of all new music)
- Spotify removed **75 million "spammy tracks"** in the previous 12 months
- One scammer made **$8 million** using AI-generated music and bot streams before pleading guilty
- AI-generated songs are being uploaded to the profiles of **dead or inactive artists** to exploit their fanbases
- Spotify launched "Artist Profile Protection" in March 2026 as a response

**Impact:** The flood of AI slop makes it **harder for legitimate AI-assisted creators to be taken seriously**. This creates demand for platforms that produce verifiably professional, human-guided output — exactly Major 72's positioning.

_Sources: [Time Magazine](https://time.com/article/2026/03/26/ai-slop-is-threatening-musicians-can-tech-companies-stem-the-tide-/), [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/24/spotify-tests-new-tool-to-stop-ai-slop-from-being-attributed-to-real-artists/), [Music Business Worldwide](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/the-ai-music-problem-on-spotify-and-other-streaming-platforms-is-worse-than-you-think/), [Digital Music News](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2026/03/29/artists-fight-back-against-ai-slop/)_

### Unmet Customer Needs

**1. Professional-Grade AI Integration (Not Replacement)**
Producers want AI that handles drudgery (stem separation, tuning, file management) while **they retain creative control**. No current tool fully delivers this. Suno replaces the producer entirely; LANDR only handles mastering. The middle ground — AI as a skilled assistant guided by professional judgment — is the unserved gap.

**2. Workflow Continuity**
File format compatibility remains a significant friction point. Producers need:
- High-quality audio stems and standard MIDI files that any DAW can read
- Seamless integration without re-entering prompts across tools
- Cross-tool orchestration instead of isolated generators

**3. The AI-to-Live Performance Pipeline**
This is a confirmed unmet need. AI music tools generate complete songs but **none of them export sheet music**. Services like Music Notation Hub have emerged to bridge this gap (human transcription of AI tracks), but no integrated platform exists. The process remains manual and fragmented:
- AI generates track → human manually transcribes → charts sent to musicians → separate hiring process
- No tool connects AI production → score generation → session musician hiring in one workflow

**4. Ethical Provenance and Copyright Clarity**
- Fully AI-generated music **cannot be copyrighted** (USCO position)
- Producers need clear proof that their human contribution makes the work copyrightable
- Training data provenance matters: two-thirds rate ethical sourcing as very important
- No platform currently provides automated "proof of human authorship" documentation

**5. Distinguishing Quality AI Work from AI Slop**
With 112,000+ tracks flooding platforms daily, legitimate AI-assisted creators need a way to signal that their work is **professionally guided, not prompt-generated garbage**. No current tool addresses this reputational need.

_Sources: [Music Notation Hub](https://musicnotationhub.com/ai-to-live-arrangement/), [Soundverse Workflow](https://www.soundverse.ai/blog/article/how-to-build-a-music-ai-workflow-for-creators-0051), [AI Journal](https://aijourn.com/ai-music-production-tools-2026-how-producers-use-ai-to-cut-friction-in-modern-music-workflows/)_

### Barriers to Adoption

**Price Barriers:**
- AI tool subscriptions ($15-$40/month) stack on top of existing DAW, plugin, sample, and distribution costs
- Producers face **subscription fatigue** — adding another $30/month tool needs to clearly replace existing spend
- Licensing AI-compliant platforms introduces new cost barriers for independent artists
- Session musician hiring remains expensive ($50+ per musician per track via Fiverr/SoundBetter)

**Technical Barriers:**
- AI outputs often need significant manual cleanup before professional use
- Integration with existing DAW workflows is still clunky — most tools are standalone web apps
- Quality inconsistency means producers can't trust AI output without review
- Steep learning curve for non-technical users who want pro results

**Trust Barriers:**
- Copyright uncertainty — unclear ownership status of AI-assisted works
- Training data ethics — major lawsuits (UMG v. Suno, Warner v. Udio) create hesitancy
- Pending fair use ruling (UMG v. Suno, expected summer 2026) could reshape the entire market
- Fear that using AI will damage professional reputation among peers

**Convenience Barriers:**
- No single platform covers the full workflow (create → produce → arrange → perform → distribute)
- Switching between tools loses context and creative momentum
- AI tools designed for non-musicians (Suno) are too simple for pros; tools designed for pros are too fragmented

_Sources: [Jam.com Copyright Guide](https://jam.com/resources/ai-music-copyright-2026), [Complete Music Update](https://completemusicupdate.com/music-and-ai-2025s-developments-that-will-shape-2026s-disputes/), [Soundverse Cost Comparison](https://www.soundverse.ai/blog/article/ai-music-vs-hiring-a-composer-cost-and-speed-comparison-0134)_

### Customer Satisfaction Gaps

**Expectation vs. Reality:**
- Users expect "type a prompt, get a hit song" — reality is generic output requiring heavy editing
- Professional producers expect DAW-level integration — reality is mostly standalone web interfaces
- Artists expect clear copyright ownership — reality is legal limbo

**Quality Gaps:**
- AI excels at short-form, simple structures but struggles with dynamic, expressive full-length tracks
- Mixing and mastering AI (LANDR) is mature; composition and arrangement AI is still immature
- Vocal synthesis has improved dramatically but still fails the "uncanny valley" test for many producers

**Value Perception Gaps:**

- The gap between "demo quality" and "release quality" AI output is where most frustration lives
- Anonymous session players who were "good enough" see decreased value, while players with **signature feels and microtiming that algorithms can't fake** see increased value

## Customer Decision Processes and Journey

### Customer Decision-Making Processes

**Three distinct buyer journeys exist in this market, each with different timelines and decision factors:**

**Journey 1: The Professional Producer (weeks to months)**
Producers treat AI tool adoption as a **workflow question, not a feature race**. The decision process is:
1. **Awareness** — Peer recommendation, YouTube demo, or industry press (Sound On Sound, MusicTech)
2. **Experimentation** — Free trial or free tier; test with a real project, not a toy exercise
3. **Evaluation** — Does the output quality meet professional standards? Does it integrate with my DAW? Does it save me time on tedious tasks?
4. **Cautious adoption** — Use for specific technical tasks (stem separation, tuning) while retaining creative control
5. **Subscription** — Only when it proves value over 2-4 weeks of use; commercial rights are the trigger

~50% of producers are still at stage 2-3 (experimental), with only ~20% as committed users. This is a **slow, trust-based conversion funnel**.

**Journey 2: The Content Creator / Hobbyist (minutes to days)**
1. **Awareness** — TikTok/Instagram viral example, YouTube comparison video
2. **Try it free** — Generate a song with text prompt (Suno, Udio)
3. **Hit the paywall** — Free credits exhausted, or need commercial rights
4. **Subscribe or leave** — $10-$30/month; conversion driven by immediate need (video deadline, content calendar)

This is a **fast, utility-driven funnel**. Suno's 2% conversion rate (2M paid / 100M total users) is typical for freemium at scale.

**Journey 3: The Gift Buyer (minutes to hours)**
1. **Trigger** — Upcoming wedding, birthday, anniversary, memorial
2. **Search** — "custom song for wedding" or "personalized song gift"
3. **Compare** — Songfinch ($200-$500+), Songlorious ($250+), CherishSong ($40+), Song Mint (AI, cheaper)
4. **Purchase** — One-time transaction; driven by price, turnaround time, and quality samples
5. **Share** — Gift is presented; viral sharing potential if experience is emotional

This is a **transactional funnel** — no subscription needed, high AOV ($50-$500), decision made in a single session.

### Strategic Recommendations

#### Recommendation 1: Lead with the Professional Producer Segment

**Why:** The largest untapped segment (~50% of producers) is cautiously experimenting with AI but hasn't committed. They want:
- Time savings on technical tasks (stem separation, tuning, mixing)
- Protection of their creative identity
- Professional-grade output quality
- Human-in-the-loop control

Major 72's Creative Modes directly address all four needs. Eddie's philosophy — "tools that save time but allow human intervention" — is precisely what this segment is waiting for.

**How:** Launch MVP as a professional tool first, not a consumer product. The professional market builds credibility; consumer follows.

#### Recommendation 2: The MVP Should Be Narrower Than the Framework Suggests

**Recommended MVP (3-6 months):**
1. Upload rough recording (bass line, vocal demo, guitar riff)
2. AI stem separation + quality enhancement
3. Apply Eddie Signature Creative Mode
4. Export polished stems + mixed/mastered track
5. "Before and after" comparison for marketing content

**Why this scope:** It proves the core thesis (professional judgment + AI = better output) with minimum engineering. Everything else — live pipeline, consumer layer, blockchain — is validated demand that can be built later.

**What to defer:**
- Session Players / AI arrangement (Phase 2)
- Score generation / live charts (Phase 2)
- Consumer personalization (Phase 3)
- SoundBetter integration (Phase 3)
- Blockchain / marketplace (Phase 4+)

#### Recommendation 3: Position Against AI Slop, Not Against Other Tools

**The AI slop crisis is your best marketing ally.** With 50,000 AI tracks flooding Deezer daily and Spotify removing 75 million spammy tracks, the market is desperate for quality differentiation.

**Recommended positioning:** "Not another AI generator. A professional production system guided by real producers."

**Avoid:** Positioning as "better than Suno" or "cheaper than Songfinch." That puts you in their category. Instead, create a new category: **"AI-guided professional production"** — where the AI does the work and the producer makes the decisions.

#### Recommendation 4: The Custom Song Market is Your Fastest Revenue Path

**The math:**
- Songfinch: $199-$399/song, 7-14 day delivery, 100% human
- Major 72 Consumer: $49-$149/song, delivery in minutes, AI + human finishing
- Songfinch tracked $75M revenue in 2023
- Capturing even 10% = $7.5M revenue with much lower COGS

**Why this matters:** The custom song market has proven demand, emotional virality (90% say it's the "most meaningful gift ever"), and high AOV. It can fund platform development while the professional tool gains traction.

**Critical design point:** The consumer flow must be a **separate brand/landing page** from the pro tool. Don't confuse "professional production platform" with "wedding song generator." Same engine, different front door.

#### Recommendation 5: Build the Eddie Calibration Process as a Repeatable Methodology

**Risk:** The entire Creative Mode system currently depends on one person. If Eddie can't calibrate, the product doesn't differentiate.

**Mitigation:**
1. Document the calibration process as a step-by-step methodology
2. Build an approve/reject UI for evaluating AI output against reference tracks
3. Make the feedback loop systematic (A/B testing, scoring rubrics)
4. Design it so other producers can eventually create their own modes
5. This becomes the foundation for the Producer Mode Marketplace (Phase 4)
